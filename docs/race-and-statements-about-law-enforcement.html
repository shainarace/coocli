COOCLI Survey Analysis

3 Race and Statements about Law Enforcement

3.1 Conclusions from this analysis

  • No significant differences across race in the overall number of statements about law Enforcement identified between blacks and non-blacks.
  • African-Americans are much more likely to report that law enforcement didn’t believe their card was theirs.
    • This result was pervasive across other county groupings and rural/urban groupings - I would not waving it away as a “Durham problem,” even though the majority of the sample-size for African-Americans does hail from Durham.
    • this finding has an interesting interaction with gender.
    • while white females are less likely than their non-female counterparts to face this type of scrutiny, black females are more likely to report it than black non-females.
  • White people are more likely to be arrested for supplies or have them confiscated. This is probably because Haywood respondents are over 80% white, and this was a big problem in Haywood.

3.2 Analysis

The variable across the columns here is the sum of the responses to the questions about law enforcement. 54% of African-Americans identified with at least one of the law enforcement questions, compared to 49% of non-African-Americans. To test the significance of this, we can use kruskal-wallis on the lawSum variable or consider a chi-square test on the binary attribute I(lawSum>0).

Figure 3.1: No significant differences across race in the overall number of statements about law Enforcement identified between blacks and non-blacks. 

## 
##  Kruskal-Wallis rank sum test
## 
## data:  lawSum by RaceAfricanAmerican
## Kruskal-Wallis chi-squared = 0.095126, df = 1, p-value = 0.7578
##

##  replicates)
##

## X-squared = 0.65859, df = NA, p-value = 0.4888

Figure 3.2: African-Americans are much more likely to report that law enforcement didn’t believe their card was theirs. 

## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test with simulated p-value (based on 2000
##  replicates)
## 
## data:  RaceAfricanAmerican and LawDidntBelieveMine
## X-squared = 6.1898, df = NA, p-value = 0.01499

This result is notable across other county groupings and rural/urban groupings - I would not feel comfortable waving it away as a “Durham problem,” even though the majority of the sample-size for African-Americans does hail from Durham.

Figure 3.3: The odds that an African-American has faced scrutiny regarding their NCHRC card are higher than other ethnicities, and this is true beyond Durham.

When we break this down across gender, we see an interaction, though we do not have a sample size sufficient to claim it’s significant.

## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test with simulated p-value (based on 2000
##  replicates)
## 
## data:  GenderFemale and LawDidntBelieveMine
## X-squared = 0.87696, df = NA, p-value = 0.4008

Figure 3.4: Black females face scrutiny at a higher rate than any other group. This demonstrates an interaction between race and gender, with white females less likely to questioned and black females more likely to face scrutiny.

Given the small sample-size, the association is still notable and should be further studied.
